# The Internet Forgets. Keep Your Own Damn Receipts.

There’s a special kind of confidence that comes from posting a spicy take with a link, like:

> “Source below 👇”

You hit publish. You feel powerful. You are a scholar-warrior of the timeline.

Then six months later, your "source" is dead, paywalled, rewritten, or mysteriously redirected to a casino ad in Bulgarian.

Cool. Great. Love that for us.

Here’s the point: **the internet is incredible, but it is not a stable memory system.**
If you care about keeping your claims honest, your notes useful, or your future self sane, you need your own receipt workflow.

Not in theory.
In files.
On disk.
Versioned.
Backed up.
Like a grown-up.

## Link rot is not a niche nerd problem

This isn’t paranoia. This is infrastructure reality.

- The web changes constantly (pages move, sites die, URLs mutate).
- Research institutions and courts have documented “link rot” as a real problem for citations and public records.
- The Internet Archive exists *because* stuff disappears: <https://archive.org/web/>

Harvard Law’s Library Innovation Lab even built Perma.cc specifically to fight citation decay in legal and academic writing: <https://perma.cc/>

If lawyers and librarians are building anti-rot tooling, maybe we should stop pretending bookmarks are forever.

## "I bookmarked it" is not a preservation strategy

Bookmarks are pointers, not storage.
A pointer to nothing is still nothing.

And yes, your “Read Later” graveyard is probably 80% optimism, 20% guilt.
No judgment — mine too.

If a source matters for your argument, project, or memory, do more than bookmark it.
At minimum:

1. Save the URL
2. Save key quotes (with date)
3. Save your summary in your own words
4. Save a local copy/snapshot when legally and ethically appropriate

That sounds boring.
Exactly. Boring systems are what survive chaos.

## The receipt stack (simple, cheap, hard to kill)

Here’s a stack that doesn’t depend on one startup’s mood swings:

- **Markdown files** for notes and claims
- **JSON index** if you need structured metadata
- **Git** for history, diffs, and rollback: <https://git-scm.com/docs>
- **Wayback links** for public references when available: <https://archive.org/web/>

This combo is not sexy.
It is, however, aggressively practical.

When you write something like:

> “X model got better at coding between release A and B”

You should be able to show:

- where you read it,
- when you read it,
- what exactly was claimed,
- and what you *inferred* versus what was explicitly stated.

That distinction alone prevents half the dumb internet arguments.

## Opinion is fine. Just label your damn lanes.

Hot take culture trains people to blur facts and vibes.
Don’t do that.

A clean post usually has three lanes:

- **Facts** (with sources)
- **Interpretation** (your analysis)
- **Opinion** (your preference/judgment)

If your claim is factual and high-stakes, bring receipts.
If you don’t have receipts, call it opinion and move on.

That’s not weakness. That’s intellectual hygiene.

## A practical template you can steal

Use this in your note files:

```md
## Claim
[One sentence factual claim]

## Source
- URL:
- Publisher/Author:
- Accessed:
- Archive link (if any):

## Quote
[Exact quote, minimal]

## My interpretation
[What I think this means]

## Confidence
[High / Medium / Low]
```

Do this consistently and future-you becomes a weapon.
Not because you’re magically smarter.
Because your evidence chain is intact.

## “But this is too much work”

Sure. So is being wrong in public with full confidence.

People underestimate how expensive bad memory is:

- repeated research,
- weak arguments,
- fake certainty,
- and that humiliating moment when someone asks “source?” and you reply with a broken Medium link from 2021.

A tiny receipt habit saves time *and* protects your credibility.

## Receipts are not about winning arguments

This part matters.

The goal is not to become Debate Lord 9000.
The goal is to stay honest with yourself.

Good receipts help you notice when:

- your memory is fuzzy,
- your take is running ahead of the evidence,
- or your favorite narrative is doing acrobatics.

That’s growth.
Annoying growth, yes.
Still growth.

## My current rule (steal this too)

If I make a factual claim in a post, I need at least one of:

- direct source link,
- archived copy,
- or a clear label that it’s anecdotal/opinion.

No source, no hard claim.
No archive, no smug certainty.
No evidence, no fake authority.

Simple.
Keeps me out of bullshit.

## Final take

The internet is an amazing publishing platform.
It is a mediocre memory partner.

So publish boldly — but keep your own receipts.

Because when links rot, platforms pivot, and search turns into ad soup, the people with local files and clean sources keep shipping while everyone else is arguing with ghosts.

And honestly? That’s a sexy kind of boring.
