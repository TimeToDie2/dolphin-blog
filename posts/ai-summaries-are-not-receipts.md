# AI Summaries Are Not Receipts (They’re Cliff Notes With Confidence)

Let’s rip the band-aid off:

If your argument is “an AI said so,” your argument is made of wet cardboard.

Useful cardboard, sure. Fast cardboard. Sometimes weirdly insightful cardboard.
But still cardboard.

I use AI constantly. I’m not anti-AI. I’m anti-bullshit.
And the current internet vibe of “paste model output, declare victory, go to lunch” is how bad info scales at lightspeed.

## The real problem isn’t AI. It’s source laziness.

The failure mode looks like this:

1. Ask model for a claim-heavy answer
2. Copy paragraph into post/thread/report
3. Add zero primary sources
4. Act shocked when details are wrong

That’s not research. That’s autocomplete cosplay.

A summary is **derivative** by definition. It compresses. It smooths edges. It drops uncertainty. Sometimes it invents confidence where none exists. Useful? Absolutely. Final authority? Hell no.

## Receipts still matter in 2026

A few boring truths worth keeping:

- The open web runs on documented standards; HTTP semantics are formalized by the IETF in RFC 9110 (published June 2022): <https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9110>
- The Wayback Machine is run by the Internet Archive, a nonprofit preserving web history: <https://web.archive.org/>
- Even Wikipedia (the internet’s most battle-tested collaborative reference project) centers verifiability via reliable sources: <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability>

If the old internet nerds, standards people, librarians, and editors all agree on one thing, maybe we should pay attention:
**claims need traceable sources.**

## What AI is actually great at

AI is great for:

- drafting structure,
- giving you candidate angles,
- translating jargon,
- generating checklists,
- finding what to verify next.

That last one is key.

Treat model output as a **lead**, not a verdict.
Like a caffeinated intern who occasionally hallucinates footnotes.

Use it to get to better questions fast.
Then do adult work: verify, cite, archive.

## The “source ladder” (don’t skip rungs)

When I’m writing anything that could be interpreted as factual, I use this ladder:

1. **Primary source** (original document, standard, filing, dataset, direct statement)
2. **Strong secondary source** (reputable reporting or analysis that points to primary)
3. **Context source** (background explainer, history, glossary)
4. **Model summary** (optional helper, never final receipt)

If your stack is “4 only,” you’re publishing vibes.

If your stack is “1 + 2 + maybe 4,” now we’re talking.

## “But I don’t have time to verify everything”

Cool. Then narrow the claim.

Instead of:

> “X definitely caused Y globally and everyone agrees.”

Say:

> “My read is that X likely influenced Y in these cases; here are the sources I used.”

That is not weakness. That’s intellectual hygiene.

Uncertainty stated clearly beats fake certainty shouted loudly.
Every damn time.

## Practical anti-bullshit workflow (15 minutes)

When you’ve got a draft from AI and don’t want to publish garbage:

### 1) Highlight hard claims
Anything with numbers, dates, legal standards, “studies show,” “experts say,” or historical facts.

### 2) Attach one receipt per hard claim
If you can’t find one, soften or remove the claim.

### 3) Archive fragile links
Drop important pages into Wayback so future-you can still verify when the original URL mutates: <https://web.archive.org/>

### 4) Label opinion as opinion
No shame in takes. Shame is pretending takes are facts.

### 5) Keep a tiny source block
At the bottom of your note/post/report, list links you actually used.
Not links that merely look smart.

## Why this matters beyond nerd purity

Bad source habits don’t just create tiny mistakes.
They create:

- false confidence,
- faster rumor spread,
- low-trust writing,
- and teams making decisions on decorative nonsense.

Once trust is cooked, recovery is expensive.
You don’t get that back with prettier formatting.

The internet already has enough loud people freelancing reality.
Don’t join them just because an LLM wrote a smooth paragraph.

## My default rule now

I’ll use AI aggressively for:

- speed,
- synthesis,
- first drafts,
- reframing.

But for factual claims, I still want receipts I can click.
If I can’t verify safely, I pivot to opinion or clearly scoped analysis.

That’s not anti-technology.
That’s pro-accountability.

## Final take

AI is a power tool.
Power tools are amazing.
Power tools also remove fingers when used like a clown.

Use models to think faster.
Use sources to stay honest.
Use archives so your receipts survive.

And if somebody says “trust me, ChatGPT said it,”
smile politely and ask for links.

If they can’t provide any, treat the claim like it was written in dry-erase marker on a moving bus.

Readable? Sure.
Reliable? Not even a little bit.
